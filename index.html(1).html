THE LENTEN PRAYER

Prayer of St. Ephraim

Said at every weekday Lenten service
Full Text with Prostrations
Each section ends with a full prostration (metania — sign of Cross, bow to the ground). Said twice at each Lenten weekday service.
O Lord and Master of my life!

Take from me the spirit of sloth,
despondency, lust of power,
and idle talk.
🙇 FULL PROSTRATION
But give rather the spirit of chastity,
humility, patience,
and love to Thy servant.
🙇 FULL PROSTRATION
Yea, O Lord and King!
Grant me to see my own errors
and not to judge my brother,
for Thou art blessed unto ages of ages. Amen.
🙇 FULL PROSTRATION
Then 12 bows from the waist, saying at each:
"O God, cleanse me a sinner."
Then repeat the entire prayer once more → 1 final prostration.
Who Was St. Ephraim?
St. Ephraim of Nisibis (c. 306–373 AD) 🌿
A deacon, theologian, poet, and hymn-writer of the 4th century Syrian Church. Called the "Harp of the Holy Spirit," he wrote thousands of hymns in Syriac. Venerated as a Doctor of the Church in East and West. His prayer became the central Lenten prayer of the entire Orthodox Church, said at every weekday service from Clean Monday through Holy Wednesday — for over 1,600 years.
The Four Vices — What We Ask God to Remove
😴 Sloth
Deep spiritual laziness — not just physical, but a paralysis of the soul that whispers "what's the point?" It is the root of all the other vices, the poisoning of spiritual energy at its source.
😔 Despondency
The fruit of sloth: hopelessness about ourselves and God's mercy. It convinces us our sins are too great, repentance useless. This is why the Church pairs fasting with the story of the Prodigal Son — the Father always runs to meet us.
👑 Lust of Power
The desire to dominate, to be right, to impose our will on others. It makes love impossible. The antidote is the Cross: "Whoever wishes to be first shall be your slave" (Matt. 20:27).
💬 Idle Talk
Not just gossip — all speech without substance: chatter, complaining, criticism. The Desert Fathers said silence is a form of fasting. Our words reveal our hearts.
The Four Virtues — What We Ask God to Give
🧠 Chastity (Whole-mindedness)
Not merely sexual purity, but soundness of mind — seeing clearly, thinking with an undivided heart, directing all energy toward God rather than scattering it. Integrity of being.
🙏 Humility
Not self-deprecation, but seeing ourselves truthfully before God. The Publican's prayer: "God, be merciful to me, a sinner." Without humility, all other virtues become occasions for pride.
⏳ Patience
The capacity to endure suffering, difficulty, and others' failures without bitterness. The fast itself is a school of patience — the body learns to wait so the soul learns to trust.
❤️ Love (Agape)
The goal of all Lenten discipline. Not sentiment, but self-giving love — preferring others to ourselves. All prayer, fasting, and almsgiving exist only to make this possible.
The Final Petition
"Grant me to see my own errors and not to judge my brother"
All four vices and virtues meet here. When I see my own sins clearly, I have no energy to judge others. And when I stop judging, I begin to love. This is the whole of repentance: turning from self to God, from self to neighbor. The prostrations unite body and soul — the whole person repents, not just the mind.
How to Pray It at Home
1. Stand before your icons. Make the sign of the Cross.
2. Read Part 1 ("Take from me…") → Full prostration.
3. Read Part 2 ("But give rather…") → Full prostration.
4. Read Part 3 ("Yea, O Lord…") → Full prostration.
5. 12 bows from the waist: "O God, cleanse me a sinner."
6. Repeat entire prayer once more → 1 final prostration.

Monday–Friday throughout Great Lent. Not on Saturdays, Sundays, or Great Feasts.
DAILY DISCIPLINE

Lenten Routine

A suggested daily rhythm for Great Lent
☀️ Morning — Upon Waking
🌅
Morning Prayers (10–15 min)
Begin with the Trisagion, then your morning prayer rule. Ask a blessing for the day. Light a candle before your icons.
🙏 Prayer
✝️
Prayer of St. Ephraim (5–10 min)
Three sections, three prostrations, twelve bows, then repeat once more. The heart of the Lenten rule. See the Prayer tab for full text.
🙏 Prayer 🙇 Prostrations
📖
Scripture Reading (10–20 min)
Read today's Old Testament passage (Isaiah + Genesis as listed in Calendar). On Sundays add the Epistle and Gospel.
📖 Reading
🍵
Break Fast — Simple & Late
Mon/Wed/Fri: ideally one meal in the evening, or nothing until noon. Tue/Thu: oil and wine permitted. Sundays: fish, wine & oil. Fast with intention, not just hunger.
🍽️ Fasting
⏸️ Midday — Work & World
📿
Jesus Prayer (throughout the day)
"Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner." Repeat as you walk, drive, wait. This is the prayer of the heart — it can be said anywhere, always.
🙏 Prayer
🤐
Fast from Idle Talk & Screens
Reduce scrolling, entertainment, gossip, complaining. Even 30 minutes of intentional silence changes the inner climate. The Desert Fathers called silence the mother of prayer.
⚡ Discipline
🤲
Almsgiving — The Third Pillar
Prayer, fasting, and almsgiving together form repentance. Each week: give something — money, time, a call to someone lonely, a meal to someone hungry. Do it quietly.
❤️ Almsgiving
🌙 Evening — Return to God
🕯️
Church Services — When Possible
Wed & Fri evenings: Liturgy of the Presanctified Gifts ★ — the most important Lenten service. Come fasting from morning. Other nights: Vespers and Compline.
⛪ Church
📚
Spiritual Reading (15–30 min)
Recommended: The Ladder of Divine Ascent (St. John Climacus) · Great Lent (Fr. Schmemann) · Life of St. Mary of Egypt · The Psalter · Lives of the Saints.
📖 Reading
🌙
Evening Prayers (10–15 min)
Evening prayer rule. Examination of conscience: Where did the four vices win today? Ask forgiveness. Then rest in peace, trusting in God's mercy.
🙏 Prayer
✝️
Prayer of St. Ephraim (evening)
Repeat the Lenten prayer at night. End your day as you began it — on your knees, with prostrations, turning wholly to God.
🙏 Prayer 🙇 Prostrations
